Losing Home

What's really at stake when we demolish public housing?

Champe Barton

Mar 2019

All styling borrowed from The Pudding
and used only for demonstration purposes.

At its peak, in 1976, the New York City Housing Authoritys Prospect Plaza in Brownsville housed an estimated 1,810 people. The quartet of red- and yellow-brick buildings had accepted its first tenants just two years before, their arrival the culmination of years of persistent and fiery activism by Brownsville tenant advocates. (In a 1971 letter to then Mayor John Lindsay, Ocean Hill-Brownsville Tenants Association representative Delores Torres referred to tenement landlords in the neighborhood as children killers.) Within a decade, the community in the twelve- and fifteen-story towers had a gravity and a mythos. Within three decades, it had an obituary.

Between 2005 and 2014, NYCHA demolished Prospect Plazathe first and only development demolished in the authoritys 85 year history.

Back then, moving into a housing project was a step up.

Roland Rich

430 Saratoga Ave. 14C

1974-1983

It was getting to the point where we had a fire once a week [in my old tenement house], so moving into Prospect was exciting. It felt like we had moved into a palace.

Lewis Anderson

430 Saratoga Ave. 12G

1975-1987

[After everybody moved out], it was a ghost town. It just felt spooky. You know, it was a graveyard here before Prospect.

Dominique Jowers

1750 Prospect Pl. 12E

1984-2002

I never thought I would see the actual building where I grew up just gone, just dirt and bricks, crumbled...it meant my grandma was really, really gone.

Vicki Colon

1750 Prospect Pl. 9E

1975-1990

Today, in Prospects place stands a squat, more modern building. The facade alternates grey and red to give the impression of several structures and on the ground floor of the building at Sterling Place and Eastern Parkway sits the carcass of a promised, but unbuilt grocery store. Where there were once 369 units of public housing there are now 80. The remaining 284 units, scattered between the new Prospect Plaza and surrounding low rise walk-ups, qualify as affordable housing: low-income apartments with income caps about three to four times higher than the typical public housing unit.

These changes fit squarely into NYCHAs departure from the welfare-state model of public housing that characterized its operations from the early seventies through the early nineties. During this period, when Roland Rich first moved into the building at 430 Saratoga Ave., NYCHA wielded public housing as an integration tool, welcoming in significantly more tenants on public assistance and relaxing the continued occupancy standards for current residents. Rich, the son of a factory worker and a home attendant, both Honduran immigrants, moved with his family to Prospect from nearby tenement houses. He remembers being so enamored by the height of his buildingand the view from his fourteenth floor apartmentthat he would sit for hours by his living room window to watch planes land at Laguardia airport. Public housing in this era, Nicholas Dagen Bloom writes in his history of the authority, Public Housing That Worked, would serve the poorest of the poor.

This is the mentality that precipitated Prospect Plazas development, and its the same mentality that sunk otherwise robust housing authorities in other cities. Chicago, for example, ultimately demolished 69 high rises as a result of its inabilitysome would argue unwillingnessto provide the maintenance required to house its most impoverished citizens. NYCHA, despite operating more than double the units of the Chicago Housing Authority in fewer square miles, demolished only Prospect Plaza.

Prospect, then, it would seem, represents the only admission of failure by the countrys largest and most successful public housing apparatus. But its history complicates that conclusion.

The reality of public housing seldom overlaps with the imaginations of people who dont live in it. Derelict, blighted towers ruled by gangs and drug dealers are far less common than well-maintained, mid-rise developments, and would have been inconceivable to the industrious, tightly-screened population who made up its earliest tenancy. As a socially-minded program enacted in 1934, a period characterized by palpable anti-socialism sentiments, public housing had to dissociate itself from the idea of the government handout. Were it not for the markets inability to rescue workers from the quicksand of the Depression, it may have taken decades longer to found a housing program at all. Under such intense scrutiny, public housings earliest tenants had to bear the dual pressures of proving the usefulness of an unorthodox system and assuaging concerns about stifled individualism. NYCHAs first tenants, at First Houses in the East Village, had to excel on a seven-pronged points system to be accepted that judged, among other categories, their employment history, rent habits, and social background.

By 1974, public housing in New York City still retained much of its earlier dignity, despite a moratorium on public housing issued by President Nixon the year before. (Nixon considered housing projects monstrous, depressing places, calling them rundown, overcrowded, [and] crime-ridden in 1973. Within months he would be impeached for obstruction of justice.) Throughout the sixties, NYCHA had managed to sustain the health of its public housing portfolio by ignoring a growing welfare population other cities were less willing to neglect; as such, the authority had yet to stain its reputation with those tenants mismanagement.

Prospect Plazas introduction to the citys public housing landscape underscores a difficult contradiction inherent in NYCHAs success. Until the early seventies, NYCHA had kept its low income housing stock alive and healthy by avoiding more radical practices aimed at serving the New Yorkers most in need. While other city housing authorities took broad, progressive action at the expense of their long-term futuresThe Chicago Housing Authority under Elizabeth Wood, for example, set aside 80% of its public housing units for African Americans, cleaving white Chicagoans support in the processNYCHA steered clear of any such decisions. Instead, conservative application of the authoritys liberal ethos ensured its survival. Prospect and the 62 NYCHA developments opened within three years of its completion would finally test the viability of a liberal approach to NYCHA management.

When Wardell and Sally Anderson applied for public housing in 1975, it was this liberal approach that made their application feasible. Wardell, who had suffered a stroke that cost him his truck driving job, worked in a window frame factory. Sally worked odd jobs and took care of the familys ten children. Lewis Anderson, seventh of the ten, remembers the application process as a swift thing, despite the potential liability of his parents work history or their number of soon-to-be-teenage sons. My mom applied for public housing, then we moved in, he remembers.

Until the mid-eighties, the Anderson family suffered little from those liabilities. Lewis played piano, and formed a band with other 430 Saratoga Ave. teenagers. His brother played the drums; Keith Porter from the eleventh floor played guitar; Monroe Williams from the seventh floor played bass. They performed at block parties and community events with one dollar admissions, covering selections from the R&B top forty. They never decided on a name, but Anderson nevertheless said he thought the group would go on to succeed Earth, Wind, and Fire.

So while drugs and crime were not absent from Prospect Plaza, neither were they omnipresent. Tenants from this period remember the community far more for its softball games than for its trauma.

Oh god [I got in trouble] a lot. Me and Mikey got caught kissing on the blocks. He was one of the Johnsons from the seventh floor.

Sunny memories grow increasingly scarce toward the dawn of the eighties. A well meaning federal amendment passed in 1969 capped rents in public housing projects at 25 percent of income, gutting NYCHA revenues. Outspoken New York Planning Official Roger Starr speculated in a 1971 editorial that if the trend to welfare tenancy continues, and the working families stay out of New York City Public Housing, there will be a consequent deterioration of the projects. He wasnt wrong. By the late seventies, an overabundance of extremely poor tenantsand thus, increasingly paltry dividends in places like Prospectmeant even maintenance, once the pearl of NYCHAs roster of services, had sagged.

[The elevators] worked maybe less than 50% of the time. Old ladies would have to wait outside in the summertime for maintenance to come.

Vicki Colon

I remember going up twelve flights of stairs with my keyboard. I would take it up to the seventh floor, rest at Monroes place, then go the other five...Imagine my brother with the drums.

I lived on the tenth floor. I kept in shape.

This period coincided with simmering racial tensions across the city and country. William Chill Sanchez, a forty-three-year old former tenant of 1786 Prospect Pl. said he never saw a white person in the neighborhood other than the police. It made life difficult for him growing up: As a light skinned Puerto Rican in a 90% black neighborhood, he recalled, I had to fight every bully.

New York Citys fiscal crisis of the nineteen-seventies disproportionately burdened African Americans, whom racist housing policies had shut out of the suburban housing boom of the fifties and sixties, and the stabler wealth that accompanied it. The proposal of a project development in predominantly white, predominantly Jewish Forest Hills, Queens in 1966 encapsulated these racial tensions: Throughout the seventies, racist community opposition in Forest Hills forced Mayor Lindsay into a series of embarrassing concessions that historians argue permanently derailed his promising political career. Prospect was not insulated from the controversy: in another of her letters to Mayor Lindsay, Delores Torres invoked the debacle to pressure the mayor into co-signing Prospects development:

As so often happens in our community, the middle-class voting minority become the so-called representatives of the majority who are poor. To a certain extent it is understandable that the people of Forest Hills do not want low-income housing. But when our own representatives use every conceivable means to block low-income housing or to see to it that only a minimum of low-income housing is built in a community where 85% of the people are in the low-income bracket, that is when it is time for the Mayor of the city to step in and see to it the majority of the population is served.

No record could be found of Mayor Lindsays response to these letters, but within a year, developers broke ground at the Prospect site.

That Prospect Plazas tenant population was nearly one hundred percent nonwhite was not inherently problematic except for the fact that, as Torres points out, it was also overwhelmingly poor. Increasingly inconsistent maintenance and turbulent race relations coalesced with this demographic concentration to produce a smoldering unrest in Prospect at the turn of the seventies. The crack epidemic would cover it in lighter fluid.

Prospect tenants generally agree that the community bifurcates into pre-crack and post-crack eras. Tenants old enough to remember the former period tend to think of their home as an upwardly mobile station; those coming of age in the latter internalized a more Nixonian assessment, their positive memories outlined in black. Those whose years straddle the divide see the forest for the trees: It went from sugar to shit, William Sanchez put it. In any case, crack hit Prospect hard, aided by towers that put drug operation lookouts a permanent step ahead of police and by a significant adolescent male population.

Roland Rich, from 430 Saratoga Ave., who had left for the army in 1983 before the epidemic took root, returned to a different Prospect in late 1985. Once friendly with every crowd, Richs lack of drug affiliation now made people wary. Nobody was giving daps anymore, he remembers. Rich avoided addiction with a turn to the Five Percent Nation, an offshoot of the Nation of Islam that culled a following in hip hop and military circles across the country (Rakim, Big Daddy Kane, and the Wu-Tang Clan are just a few of the rap icons who count themselves Five Percenters). He and his best friends, Derrick Rahking Davis and Dexter Infinite Fortner, spent Tuesdays and Thursdays studying Five Percent lessons at the community center, and weekends studying at a building on Dean Street. Rich, now fifty-six can still recite many of the movements 120 lessons, and does so with marrow-deep pride. He attributes some of the chaos of the crack era to frustrations boiling over, and to dozens of young men unwilling or unable to channel those frustrations as he had into his commitment to the Five Percent lessons. We gotta deal with police brutality, then we gotta deal with racism outside our communities, then we gotta deal with violence and black-on-black crime within them, he explained. We were angry.

Much of the news coverage during this period, rightfully criticized in retrospect for its callous treatment of the mostly black victims of an addiction epidemic, confirmed the public perception of housing as a last resort for the citys poorest class. To an increasing degree, white New Yorkers conflated public housing with minority housing. As a result, white applications for admission to public housing plummeted in the eighties. By 1995, whites constituted just 8% of NYCHA tenancy, down from 29% in 1971. This was a problem for a program whose survival depended on widespread public support from a majority-white public, and whose bottom-line depended on a significant working-class tenant base. It would ultimately cause problems for Prospect as well, whose public image by the mid-nineties was enshrined most visibly in the killing of a four-year-old roller skater at the entryway to 1750 Prospect Pl.

A declining revenue stream and a grim national picture of public housing by the early nineties (Chicago Mayor Jane Byrne, after moving to the troubled Cabrini-Green projects to draw attention to their plight, called the project a cancer that can spread to every neighborhood in the cityshe ended her stay less than a month later) helps explain why NYCHA decided to participate in the Department of Housing and Urban Developments HOPE VI program. HOPE VI was designed, on recommendation from the Final Report of the National Commission on Severely Distressed Public Housing, to grant federal funds to local housing authorities to demolish or revitalize the worst of their housing stock. HUD ultimately aimed at tweaking the income balance in extremely-low-income areas, thus facilitating a movement away from the strategies that had hamstrung housing authorities across the U.S. in years past.

Without many significantly dilapidated developments to tend to, however, NYCHA seldom utilized HOPE VI funds. But in 1996 and 1997, grants were proposed for two projects, respectively: the Arverne/Edgemere Houses in Queens and Prospect Plaza.

At this time, a man named Milton Bolton ran Prospect Plazas tenants association. Bolton had moved to Prospect just two years before, settling into his ninth floor Saratoga Ave. apartment with something less than gratitude. It never felt like a home to me, he explained matter-of-factly during a recent interview in his car outside of a Starbucks. I figured, give me six months, and Ill be out of here. His contempt for the developments conditions motivated a run for tenants association president, and with a disinterested tenancy, he ran unopposed.

But since HUD intended HOPE VI as a collaborative effort, they made Bolton, who had yet to live all of two years at the development, their point man for Prospect Plaza tenants. He would need to seek approval from his constituency, and then work closely with NYCHA on behalf of that constituency to articulate a clear and compelling vision for the $21.4 million revitalization grant offered. He would need to field and focus concerns voiced by tenants who had lived in the building longer than he, some by decades. And he would need to represent this constituency long after his position expired. In the early two thousand tens, years after the tenants association dissolved, the New York Times pitched Bolton as the de facto voice of Prospect Plaza.

I moved from 155 Workman Avenue. It was like a palace compared to Prospect.

Milton Bolton

430 Saratoga Ave. 9E

1996-2003

Bolton felt that tenants who had been at Prospect longer than him and let the place deteriorate didnt deserve to run the tenants association. He subscribed to a Cosby-esque theory of social responsibility, attributing little of the communitys late-nineties disarray to historical tailwinds and much of it to individual shortcomings. One flyer dispersed to tenants involved in the HOPE VI process featured a picture of Bolton and began, When are we going to learn? It proceeded to criticize black male figures who resided exclusively in the crack-houses, jail-houses and the liquor stores.

For all his moralizing, Bolton was proactive. He sent reams of letters to NYCHA and HUD officials, and by the end of 1998, all parties had agreed upon a proposal that would see the demolition of the high rise on 430 Saratoga Ave. and a gut renovation of the remaining three buildings on Prospect Place. Between 1999 and 2003, tenants would temporarily relocate to other NYCHA properties or use Section 8 vouchers to move elsewhere. NYCHA Chairman Tino Hernandez, in a 2002 letter to tenants, assured them a space in their newly renovated home. All residents, he wrote, have the absolute right to return to the development once construction is complete, so long as you remain in good standing at your new location.

According to documents from the period, NYCHA waffled on a definitive timeline for tenants return. In 2005, they demolished the tower on 430 Saratoga Ave., but hadnt loosened a screw in any of the three towers on Prospect Place. Disagreements between Michaels Development Co., the developer contracted for the renovation work, and NYCHA came to a head in 2007, when NYCHA terminated their contract citing financial feasibility concerns. Michaels, in April of the following year, sued NYCHA for breach of contract. The case was settled with neither entity paying injuries.

The breakup left tenants in limbo; without a developer contracted to renovate their apartments, and with public acknowledgement from NYCHA that doing so proved more costly than the authority would tolerate, their likelihood of return slimmed. In addition, a 1998 federal law binded NYCHA to fill half of the vacancies in any public housing development with working class families, making between $31,450 and $66,550 a year. NYCHA public housing families earned, on average, $9,704, according to a 1999 Housing and Vacancy survey. Whether this was shrewd calculus to hammer the nail in the coffin of welfare-state housing, or simply an unhappy coincidence for Prospect Plaza tenants whose homes, upon failed background check, NYCHA could consider vacant, is unclear.

NYCHA did not respond to multiple requests for comment.

HUD designed these temporary relocations to grow less temporary with time. HOPE VI explicitly aimed at deconcentration of poverty in communities like Prospects, meaning the programs success hinged on a good portion of tenants either choosing not to return or being denied that choice. An Urban Institute study of the HOPE VI program in 2003 found that, including non-public housing households, an average of 41 percent of current HOPE VI residents lived in the developments prior to revitalization. In other words, on average, over half of residents whose developments made use of HOPE VI money did not return home.

In 2010, a decade now after the first tenants left Prospect Plaza, NYCHA made this pattern explicit. In a revised revitalization proposal, the authority revealed plans to demolish the remaining three towers and replace them with townhouses and mid-rise walk-ups. Only 80 public housing units would survive; the remainder, classified as affordable, would accept tenants with higher incomes and more favorable work histories. Viewed another way, Prospect residents would reap little of the benefit they had helped sow throughout the redevelopment process. Instead, they overwhelmingly faced new living situations as racially and economically homogeneous as Prospects, a pattern that prompted Fair Housing Act violations in Baltimore around the same time. A majority of Prospect tenants had moved to census tracts where over half the population was black (compared to 26 percent citywide) and where roughly a third of the population was below the poverty line (compared to 19.5 percent citywide)

Sharon Ebert, NYCHAs director of Housing Finance and Development from 1999 to 2003, says tenants chose these arrangements, given the option to select between NYCHA properties with vacancies or Section 8 vouchers. But her explanation belies a deeper criticism. For all the praise lauded on NYCHA over the decades for steady, conservative integration efforts, its success really only shines in the dull glow of lesser authorities. Tenants, given the option to move to any NYCHA property, still ended up in areas overwhelmingly black and poor. It may not have been as bad as Baltimore, but New York City was hardly Montclair.

NYCHA completed demolition of 1786 Prospect Pl., 1765 Prospect Pl. and 1750 Prospect Pl., in October of 2014. It would take an additional four years before doors opened at the new Prospect Plaza, and only a handful of former tenants, after an eighteen-year wait, would make it back. Dominique Jowers was one of them. For the past eight years, she sat through every update meeting, participated in every planning consult, and answered every letter and email about the coming development to be sure she reserved her spot. Now, in her spotless first floor apartment at the intersection of Howard Ave. and Prospect Pl., she said shes grateful she gets to share the memories of her childhood with her son. She beamed: Its like Im passing on a tradition.

As for how the new development compares to the old, she said her current apartment makes her feel like shes in an out-of-town condo. She could name half of her old buildings residents from memory, but doesnt know many people at the new Prospect. She still makes a point to decorate the halls for the seasons. She said there are no benches in front of the building, to prevent people from gathering outside as they often did before the demolition. All in all, she said its much quieter now. One wonders if that was the point.
